# 5. Evaluation

Exploration is expected to lower short-term engagement by construction. If
you measure it on session click-through rate you will always kill it. The
evaluation stack must cover three distinct things: how a new policy would
have performed on historical data (offline), how well the system is learning
(regret), and whether exploration pays off in the real product (online).

## Offline: off-policy evaluation

You cannot A/B test every candidate policy; it is too slow and too expensive.
Off-policy evaluation (OPE) estimates how a new policy would have performed
using logs generated by the old (logging) policy.

**Replay evaluation** is the simplest and the most trustworthy, but it
requires a special log. You need a portion of traffic where impressions were
served uniformly at random. On that traffic, replay the new policy: score
each logged event, and count only the events where the new policy's choice
matches the logged action. The resulting win rate is an unbiased estimate of
the new policy's value. Yahoo used this method on 33 million events to
evaluate LinUCB before it touched live traffic.

The cost: replay burns most of the log. Because the new policy only matches
a fraction of logged actions, most events are discarded. You need a large
volume of uniformly-random traffic to get a low-variance estimate.

**Inverse propensity scoring (IPS)** reuses any logged traffic where
propensities are known and nonzero, not just uniform random traffic. It takes
as input a log of events $(x_i, a_i, r_i)_{i=1}^n$ with known logging
propensities $\pi_0(a_i \mid x_i)$ and a candidate policy $\pi$; it outputs
a scalar estimate of the expected per-round reward the new policy would have
earned on that traffic.

$$\hat{V}_{\text{IPS}}(\pi) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\pi(a_i \mid x_i)}{\pi_0(a_i \mid x_i)} \, r_i$$

Here $\pi$ is the new policy, $\pi_0$ is the logging policy, $a_i$ is the
action taken, $x_i$ is the context, and $r_i$ is the observed reward. The
ratio reweights each event so the new policy is effectively simulated on the
old log. IPS is unbiased if every action had nonzero logging probability,
but the variance explodes when the ratio is large (the new policy assigns
high probability to actions the old policy rarely took).

**Doubly-robust (DR)** adds a learned reward model $\hat{r}$ as a baseline
and importance-weights only its residual:

$$\hat{V}_{\text{DR}}(\pi) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left[ \hat{r}(x_i, \pi) + \frac{\pi(a_i \mid x_i)}{\pi_0(a_i \mid x_i)} \Bigl( r_i - \hat{r}(x_i, a_i) \Bigr) \right]$$

The estimate stays consistent if either the reward model or the propensities
are correct (but not if both are wrong). Instacart uses IPS and doubly-robust
together before any A/B, specifically to hedge against a bad reward model
or inaccurate propensities.

**Hard requirement from all three:** the propensities logged at serve time
must exactly match the policy that ran. A mismatch silently corrupts every
estimator. Teams should test this directly by replaying logged random traffic
and verifying the estimator recovers known outcomes.

## Regret: measuring how fast the policy learns

Regret is the gap between the cumulative reward the policy earned and the
cumulative reward an oracle (knowing all true arm values) would have earned.
Over $T$ rounds:

$$\text{Regret}(T) = \sum_{t=1}^{T}\bigl(\mu^{\ast} - \mu_{a_t}\bigr), \quad \mu^{\ast} = \max_a\, \mu_a$$

where $\mu^{\ast}$ is the mean reward of the best arm and $\mu_{a_t}$ is the mean
reward of the arm chosen at round $t$. Lower cumulative regret over time means the policy learned faster and paid
a smaller cost for exploration.

- **Linear regret:** the policy never learns; the exploration cost compounds
  indefinitely (epsilon-greedy with a fixed epsilon on a stationary problem
  approaches this).
- **Logarithmic regret:** the policy learns efficiently; UCB and Thompson
  sampling achieve this on well-behaved reward distributions.

Regret is primarily an offline or simulation metric; it is hard to measure
directly on a live system because you do not observe the oracle's cumulative
reward.

## Online: what to measure in a live experiment

An online A/B on exploration looks different from a standard A/B.

| Metric | What it measures | Gotcha |
|---|---|---|
| Short-term engagement (CTR, dwell) | immediate user satisfaction | exploration lowers this by construction; do not kill exploration here |
| New-item impression share | fraction of impressions going to recently uploaded items | the first sign that exploration is working; a rising share means cold items are getting a fair shot |
| Corpus diversity | number of distinct items in the feed over a rolling window | the ossification metric; a shrinking number is the problem we are solving |
| Long-horizon retention | 7-day, 30-day return rate | the true payoff; must be measured over a long window, not just the first session |
| Propensity calibration | does the logged propensity match the true policy probability? | run as a pre-launch sanity check, not just at experiment end |

The guardrail: exploration is expected to cause a small short-term engagement
dip. Ship if long-horizon retention or corpus diversity improves, and the
short-term dip is within the acceptable floor. Never judge exploration solely
on session CTR.

## When to use which evaluation method

| Reach for | When | Instead of |
|---|---|---|
| Replay off-policy eval | the log carries uniformly-random exploration traffic (a dedicated explore bucket) | IPS on a near-deterministic log, where the variance explodes |
| IPS | any log with known nonzero propensities; you have a reasonable logging policy | replay, when you cannot afford a large random-explore bucket |
| Doubly-robust | you want to hedge against either a bad reward model or imperfect propensities | IPS alone when you are confident in both |
| Long-horizon A/B (retention, corpus) | the final launch decision on an exploration feature | session-level CTR alone, which is structurally biased against exploration |
| Regret in simulation | comparing exploration policies in a controlled environment before live deployment | live A/B for every candidate policy |

**Tools.** Replay, IPS, and doubly-robust estimators are implemented in the Open Bandit Pipeline (obp) and in Vowpal Wabbit's policy-evaluation mode, both of which consume a log of contexts, actions, propensities, and rewards. The doubly-robust baseline reward model is any scikit-learn or PyTorch (Meta) regressor. Regret comparisons run in a lightweight custom simulator or a bandit environment; the final long-horizon A/B on retention and corpus diversity leans on the platform's experiment stack.

**Worked example.** A streaming service vetting a new exploration policy before it touches live traffic starts with off-policy evaluation on its logs. If it maintains a dedicated bucket of uniformly-random explore traffic, it uses replay for an unbiased, trustworthy estimate; where it only has general logs with known nonzero propensities, it uses IPS, and adds a doubly-robust estimator to hedge against either a bad reward model or imperfect propensities, all via the Open Bandit Pipeline. It compares candidate policies by regret in a simulator rather than spinning up a live A/B for each one. Only the finalist goes to a long-horizon A/B measuring retention and corpus diversity, never session CTR alone, which is structurally biased against exploration. Throughout, it verifies that logged propensities match the policy that actually ran, since a mismatch silently corrupts every estimator.
